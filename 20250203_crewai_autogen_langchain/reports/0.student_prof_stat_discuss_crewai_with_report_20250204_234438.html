Confidence Intervals: In conclusion, confidence intervals are statistical tools used to estimate a population parameter based on a sample of data. They provide a range of values within which the true population parameter is likely to lie with a certain level of confidence. A confidence interval is defined as an interval that contains the value of a population parameter with a specified probability (usually 95%). The formula for constructing a confidence interval is given by: \[ \bar{x} - z_{\alpha/2} \times \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} < \mu < \bar{x} + z_{\alpha/2} \times \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \] where: - \( \bar{x} \) is the sample mean, - \( s \) is the sample standard deviation, - \( n \) is the sample size, - \( z_{\alpha/2} \) is the critical value from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the desired confidence level (e.g., 95% confidence level corresponds to a two-tailed test with a significance level of 0.05), - \( \mu \) is the population mean. The choice of confidence level and sample size determine the width of the interval. A higher confidence level requires a wider interval, while a smaller sample size results in a narrower interval. Confidence intervals are useful for making predictions about future outcomes by extrapolating past data to estimate the range of possible values within which an underlying population parameter will lie. The use of confidence intervals helps researchers to: 1. Make informed decisions based on empirical evidence. 2. Draw conclusions from their findings, rather than relying solely on p-values. 3. Conduct hypothesis testing and determine the significance of their results.
